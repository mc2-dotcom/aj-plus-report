AJ+ and the Anti-American Editorial Line

An analysis of 13,930 tweets and 4,107 Instagram posts reveals how Qatar's Al Jazeera digital brand frames the United States as a hypocritical global actor — and how that framing surges around Gaza, U.S. elections, and the 2026 Iran war.

01 — Overview

Executive Summary

AJ+, the digital media brand launched by Qatar's Al Jazeera Media Network in 2014, has grown into one of the most prolific and widely viewed news publishers on social media — accumulating 2.2 billion Facebook video views in 2015 alone and ranking as the platform's sixth-largest video publisher that year.1

A comprehensive analysis of 13,930 AJ+ tweets (accumulating over 615 million views) and 4,107 Instagram posts reveals a consistent pattern of content that frames the United States as a hypocritical global actor — one that commits war crimes abroad, enables Israeli military operations in Gaza, and fails to uphold its own democratic ideals at home. AJ+'s single most-viewed tweet, with 46.6 million views, accused the U.S. of refusing to "take accountability for the war crimes it committed in Hiroshima and Nagasaki."2 Its top 10 tweets by viewership have a combined reach of approximately 111 million views. On Instagram, AJ+'s most-viewed post (18.5 million views) showed the clothes of 11,500 children killed in Gaza, while its most-liked post — featuring U.S. Army veterans criticizing American foreign policy at a Senate hearing — garnered 681,501 likes.33a

This anti-American and anti-Western editorial thread is not incidental. It spans content about U.S. military history, American democratic legitimacy, Western colonialism, and above all, U.S. support for Israel during the Gaza conflict. AJ+ content surges around major events — the October 2023 Gaza escalation (when Instagram engagement spiked roughly 10x), the 2024 presidential election, the Biden–Trump transition, and most recently the U.S.–Israeli military strikes on Iran in early 2026 — though the sharpest anti-American framing tends to be event-adjacent rather than precisely timed to a single day like Election Day itself. During the Iran war (March–April 2026), AJ+ produced dozens of posts framing the U.S. as a war criminal — including extensive coverage of a school bombing in Minab, Iran attributed to U.S. forces that killed 165 schoolgirls, destruction of UNESCO heritage sites, and comparisons to the 2003 Iraq WMD justification.414243

The U.S. Department of Justice ordered AJ+'s parent entity to register as a foreign agent under FARA in September 2020, an order that Al Jazeera has not complied with as of this writing.45 A 2024 analysis by the cybersecurity firm Cyabra found that 32% of profiles engaging with AJ+'s X (formerly Twitter) accounts were identified as fake, raising questions about the authenticity of its amplified reach.6

These findings draw on primary social media data, independent trade press reporting, U.S. government filings, and analyses from think tanks and watchdog organizations — several of which operate from pro-Israel advocacy positions and whose interpretive characterizations are clearly attributed throughout this report. AJ+ describes itself as "a unique, global digital news and storytelling brand dedicated to human rights and equality, holding power to account, and amplifying the voices of the unheard,"7 and Al Jazeera has contested FARA registration as an infringement on press freedom.8

02 — Headline Findings

Key Findings
F-01

AJ+'s most viral content consistently frames the United States as a war criminal or hypocritical power.

Verified

The single highest-viewed AJ+ tweet (46.6 million views) accuses the U.S. of committing "war crimes" at Hiroshima and Nagasaki and refusing accountability.2 The second-highest (11.0 million views) highlights British colonialism in the Chagos Islands.9 Among the top 10 tweets by views (combined: ~111 million), the dominant themes are Western military violence, colonial legacy, and Palestinian suffering under Israeli operations.2910

F-02

Gaza/Israel content dominates AJ+'s most-viewed Instagram posts, with humanitarian framing that implicitly critiques U.S. policy.

Strong

AJ+'s all-time most-viewed Instagram post (18.5M views, February 2024) displayed the clothes of 11,500 children killed in Gaza — a UK activist visualization.3a The third most-viewed (13.8M views) showed protesters blocking a U.S. military cargo ship heading to Israel in Oakland.44 Virtually all of AJ+'s top 20 Instagram posts by view count relate to the Israel–Gaza conflict, with content centering Palestinian civilian suffering — families fleeing bombardment, children in distress, and communications blackouts. The October 2023 escalation triggered a roughly 10x spike in Instagram engagement: average views jumped from ~165,000/post in September 2023 to ~1.6 million/post in October 2023, and average likes surged from ~5,300 to ~34,000.31112

F-03

AJ+ content surged around the 2024 U.S. presidential election and the 2026 U.S.–Iran war, though anti-American framing was distributed across these periods rather than spiking on a single day.

Strong

Between November 1–15, 2024, AJ+ published tweets featuring Muslim Americans expressing fear under a "Democratic presidency" (345,529 views), Palestinians calling the election "one criminal passing the torch to the next" (199,846 views), and an Election Day post declaring "New president, same story" (44,381 views).13141516 During the U.S.–Israeli strikes on Iran (March–April 2026), AJ+ produced at least 20 explicitly anti-American posts on Instagram alone, led by content celebrating Spain for refusing to let the U.S. use its bases for "illegal attacks on Iran" (4.1M views, 444,020 likes) and a Spanish politician calling out "hypocrisy" in using feminism to support U.S. bombing (4.8M views, 330,221 likes).4142

F-04

The U.S. Department of Justice ordered AJ+ to register under FARA in 2020; Al Jazeera has not complied.

Verified

On September 14, 2020, the DOJ sent a formal determination letter to AJ+'s legal counsel requiring registration under the Foreign Agents Registration Act.4 Al Jazeera publicly condemned the order as an attack on press freedom.8 As of 2026, no FARA registration has been filed. Bipartisan Congressional letters in 2018, 2019, 2020, and 2021 urged enforcement.517

F-05

Nearly one-third of profiles engaging with AJ+'s X accounts were identified as fake.

Verified (with source caveats)

A December 2024 investigation by Cyabra, commissioned by the Combat Antisemitism Movement, analyzed AJ+ X accounts from September 17 to November 17, 2024, and found 32% of engaging profiles were fake — broken down as 22% for @ajplus, 42% for @ajplusarabi, and 35% for @ajplusfrancais.6 The underlying data comes from Cyabra, a recognized commercial cybersecurity firm, though the study was commissioned by an advocacy organization.

F-06

AJ+ Arabic published a Holocaust denial video in 2019 that went viral before deletion.

Verified

In May 2019, AJ+ Arabic published a video questioning established facts of the Holocaust and describing Israel as the "biggest winner." Al Jazeera suspended two journalists involved and removed the video.18

F-07

AJ+ has achieved extraordinary reach as a social media news publisher.

Strong

AJ+ received 2.2 billion Facebook video views in 2015 and a total reach of 7.4 billion that year, ranking as Facebook's sixth-largest video publisher.11920 By May 2019, AJ+'s Facebook following (11.1 million) surpassed that of the Washington Post (6.3 million).17 On Twitter/X alone, AJ+'s 13,930 tracked posts have accumulated over 615 million total views at an average of ~44,000 views per post. On Instagram, AJ+'s top 15 posts have a combined view count exceeding 153 million. The Zachor Legal Institute estimated AJ+ had accumulated "over 17 billion collective views" by March 2023, a figure that — while not independently verified — is broadly consistent with the confirmed growth trajectory.5

F-08

The U.S.–Israeli war on Iran (2026) produced AJ+'s most concentrated anti-American content since October 2023.

Strong

Beginning February 28, 2026, AJ+ published at least 20 explicitly anti-American posts on Instagram alone during the Iran strikes, with recurring themes of U.S. war crimes (school bombings, cultural site destruction), Iraq WMD parallels, and celebration of countries opposing U.S. actions. The top-performing Iran-war posts reached 4.8 million views and 444,020 likes. On Twitter, the most-viewed Iran-war anti-American tweet reached 64,723 views, reflecting the platform's declining reach compared to Instagram.41424345

03 — Evidence

Detailed Analysis

I. Thematic Architecture of Anti-American Content

AJ+'s anti-American and anti-Western content operates across several distinct but reinforcing thematic lanes.

A. U.S. Military History as War Crimes

The most potent example is AJ+'s Hiroshima/Nagasaki narrative. The tweet that became AJ+'s most-viewed post of all time (46.6 million views, April 1, 2024) was pegged to the Japanese release of the film Oppenheimer: "Oppenheimer has been released in Japan, eight months after its global release. We spoke to historian Naoko Wake to learn why the U.S. refuses to take accountability for the war crimes it committed in Hiroshima and Nagasaki and why all of this is relevant to us today."2 The news-pegging of a historical accountability argument to a pop-culture moment is a common editorial technique in digital media; its effectiveness here is evidenced by the tweet's extraordinary reach — more than four times AJ+'s next most-viewed tweet.

This was not a one-off editorial choice. AJ+ returned to the Hiroshima war-crimes framing at least eight additional times around the August 2025 80th anniversary of the bombings, each post reinforcing the characterization of the U.S. atomic attacks as unacknowledged war crimes.2

B. Western Colonialism and Hypocrisy

AJ+'s second most-viewed tweet (10.96 million views, 98,768 likes, 30,149 retweets) highlighted British colonialism in the Chagos Islands.9 A February 2026 tweet sarcastically captioned "U.S. Secretary of State teaches us why colonialism was wonderful" received 21,038 views and 1,123 likes.21 AJ+ also posted a Hugo Chávez retrospective (914,845 views) timed to coincide with Trump's September 2025 UNGA speech, featuring Chávez calling George W. Bush "the devil" and "slamming U.S. hypocrisy" at the same podium in 2006.22

"[Al Jazeera's media strategy] exploits freedom of speech in the West to criticize these countries, while broadcasting from a country that boasts only limited freedom of the press." — Institute for National Security Studies (INSS), 202423

C. "Double Standards" in the Israel–Gaza Context

Two tweets from October 9–11, 2023 — posted in the immediate aftermath of the October 7 Hamas attack — framed Western responses as exhibiting a "double standard," accumulating approximately 972,000 combined views.10 This framing became a throughline in AJ+'s subsequent coverage of the Gaza war.

The Committee for Accuracy in Middle East Reporting and Analysis (CAMERA), a pro-Israel media watchdog, argued that AJ+ itself embodies hypocrisy, noting that "AJ+ championed women's rights and LGBT rights while being owned by the government of a country that doesn't grant women equal rights or recognize LGBT rights."24

D. U.S. Democracy as Fundamentally Flawed

AJ+'s editorial director Tony Karon published the outlet's 2020 election coverage plan on Election Day (November 3, 2020), framing American democracy as structurally compromised by "white minority rule" and "constitutional brakes to limit democracy."25 This editorial lens — treating American democracy not as an imperfect but aspirational system but as a fundamentally illegitimate one — recurred in the 2024 election cycle, as detailed below.

III. Timing with U.S. Political Events

2024 Presidential Election (November 1–15)

AJ+ published a cluster of content around the 2024 election that framed American democracy as morally compromised — particularly by U.S. policy on Gaza:

  • November 1: A Muslim American town hall featured the quote: "I have never felt more afraid for myself as a Muslim than I do right now under a Democratic presidency" (345,529 views).13 A separate post on Arab American voters in Michigan included: "Genocide is a red line for me" (28,211 views).14
  • November 3: Palestinians were quoted saying: "For us, it's one criminal passing the torch to the next" (199,846 views).15
  • November 5 (Election Day): AJ+ posted "New president, same story" (44,381 views) and a clip of a State Department spokesperson laughing during a press briefing (27,014 views).1632
  • November 7: A Latin American analyst discussed U.S. "fragile democracy" on Instagram (201,630 views).33
  • November 8: Coverage of the Amsterdam/Maccabi Tel Aviv incident (79,705 likes).34

Notably, the highest-viewed AJ+ tweets from the November 1–15 period were about Israeli actions in Gaza (3.6 million and 1.5 million views), not election-specific content. Anti-American framing was present throughout but did not exhibit a dramatic, singular spike on Election Day itself — instead maintaining a steady editorial posture across the two-week window.

Biden–Trump Transition (January 2025)

AJ+ bracketed the presidential transition with pointed content:

  • January 16, 2025: A post on Secretary Blinken's farewell press conference (462,678 views, 33,277 likes).35
  • January 17, 2025: A Biden legacy post (778,481 views, 45,589 likes).36

Trump UNGA Address (September 2025)

AJ+ posted a Hugo Chávez retrospective (914,845 views) timed to Trump's September 24, 2025 UNGA speech, inviting viewers to revisit Chávez's 2006 performance at the same podium where he "slammed U.S. hypocrisy and called George W. Bush 'the devil.'"22

Hiroshima 80th Anniversary (August 2025)

AJ+ published at least eight posts around the 80th anniversary of the Hiroshima and Nagasaki bombings in August–September 2025, each reinforcing the framing of U.S. atomic attacks as potential war crimes — extending the narrative thread of its most viral tweet from April 2024.2

U.S.–Israeli Strikes on Iran (March–April 2026)

The most recent — and among the most sustained — anti-American content surge occurred during the U.S.–Israeli military campaign against Iran beginning February 28, 2026. AJ+ produced dozens of posts across both platforms framing the U.S. as a lawless aggressor committing war crimes. Key content threads included:

  • Minab school bombing: Extensive multi-post coverage of a strike on a primary school in Minab, Iran that killed 165 schoolgirls. AJ+ cited Bellingcat and Reuters analyses attributing the strike to U.S. forces, with posts including a mother's testimony ("Mom, take a picture of me" — her last interaction with her son), a mass funeral, and a statement that "over 200 children" had been killed. Instagram posts on this topic garnered 48,566 likes on the mass funeral post alone.4143
  • Iraq WMD parallel: Tweets drew explicit comparisons to the 2003 Iraq invasion: "U.S. attacked Iran claiming nuclear weapons — it doesn't have them," paired with reminders that "only Israel" possesses nuclear weapons in the region.45
  • Cultural destruction: Coverage of U.S./Israeli strikes damaging Tehran's Golestan Palace (a UNESCO World Heritage site) and a historical synagogue destroyed during Passover — with a Tehran rabbi quoted: "Israeli regime's claim about defending Jews is nothing more than a shameful lie" (24,621 likes on Instagram).4647
  • "Double standard" framing: Trump was mocked for promising to end "forever wars" while launching military action on eight countries during his second term.48 A post on the Pope's Palm Sunday speech contrasted it with the U.S. Defense Secretary praying for "overwhelming destruction" in Iran.49
  • Spain as moral counterpoint: The two highest-performing Iran-war Instagram posts celebrated Spain's refusal to allow the U.S. to use shared military bases (4.1M views, 444K likes) and a Spanish politician calling out "hypocrisy" in using feminist rhetoric to justify bombing Iranian girls (4.8M views, 330K likes).4142
  • Humanitarian framing: A post on aid entering Gaza dropping ~80% "since U.S. and Israel launched war on Iran" (48,550 views on Twitter) connected the Iran campaign to Palestinian suffering.50

This content cluster represents AJ+'s most direct and concentrated anti-American messaging since October 2023, and is notable for framing the United States — not merely Israel — as the primary perpetrator of war crimes.

IV. Institutional and Regulatory Context

FARA and Congressional Pressure

The DOJ's September 2020 FARA determination found that AJ+ "acts as an agent of the government of Qatar" and required registration.4 The New York Times reported the order on September 15, 2020,4 and Al Jazeera publicly condemned it as politically motivated.8 Bipartisan Congressional letters urging enforcement were sent in 2018, 2019, 2020, and 2021, including a 2019 letter from Senator Marco Rubio and colleagues to Attorney General William Barr.175

Al Jazeera has not registered under FARA. Mother Jones noted at the time that the order came during the Trump administration and amid shifting Gulf geopolitics, raising questions about whether the action was driven by legal merits or political considerations.37

Bot Amplification

Cyabra's December 2024 investigation, commissioned by the Combat Antisemitism Movement, found that 32% of profiles engaging with AJ+'s X accounts were fake — with the Arabic-language account (@ajplusarabi) showing the highest rate at 42%.6 The Foundation for Defense of Democracies (FDD) characterized this as AJ+ "using bots to spread propaganda while skirting U.S. law," though the full FDD analysis could not be accessed for independent verification.38 The underlying Cyabra data has been confirmed.

Historical Precedents

Anti-American bias concerns at Al Jazeera predate AJ+. David Marash, who served as the Washington, D.C., anchor for Al Jazeera English, resigned in 2008 citing a "reflexive adversarial editorial stance against Americans" that he described as "so stereotypical, so reflexive."39 Then-Secretary of State Colin Powell publicly criticized Al Jazeera for doing "such a horrible job presenting the news" and taking "every opportunity to slant the news, present it in the most outrageous way."39

V. AJ+'s Self-Characterization

AJ+ describes itself as dedicated to "human rights and equality, holding power to account, and amplifying the voices of the unheard."7 Its content categories include Climate Crisis, Culture, Election, Good Trouble, (in)Justice, Solutions, and World — framing its work as progressive, social-justice-oriented journalism rather than state-directed messaging. Al Jazeera has consistently argued that FARA registration would constitute an unacceptable infringement on press freedom and would set a dangerous precedent for foreign media operating in the United States.8

04 — Evidence Base

Source Analysis

Source Reliability Overview

This report draws on four categories of evidence, listed in descending order of reliability:

Primary social media data

Post text, engagement metrics, timestamps, and view counts drawn directly from AJ+'s public social media accounts. This constitutes the report's evidentiary backbone and is independently verifiable. All specific metrics cited have been cross-checked.

Independent journalism and trade press

Reporting from the New York Times,4 BBC News,18 Digiday,1 Variety,19 Mother Jones,37 and the Chicago Tribune40 provides neutral, professionally edited coverage. These sources are rated STRONG.

U.S. government records

The DOJ FARA determination letter17 and Congressional correspondence are official documents. While political motivations may inform their creation, their factual content is authoritative.

Think tank and advocacy analyses

Several sources that have shaped public discourse about AJ+ operate from explicit institutional positions:

  • INSS (Institute for National Security Studies): Israeli national security think tank affiliated with Tel Aviv University. Produces rigorous analysis, but its perspective on Qatar's media strategy reflects Israeli strategic interests.23
  • CAMERA (Committee for Accuracy in Middle East Reporting and Analysis): Pro-Israel media watchdog.2439
  • Zachor Legal Institute: Pro-Israel legal advocacy organization. Its 2023 report on AJ+ is detailed but advocacy-oriented.5
  • Combat Antisemitism Movement / Cyabra: CAM is an advocacy organization that commissioned the bot analysis; Cyabra is a recognized commercial cybersecurity firm.6
  • FDD (Foundation for Defense of Democracies): National security think tank with a hawkish, pro-Israel orientation.38

Throughout this report, characterizations originating from advocacy sources — such as "propaganda," "designed to delegitimize," or "fanning the flames" — are attributed to their source organizations rather than presented as independent conclusions. The primary social media data and engagement metrics stand on their own.

Gaps in Evidence

  • AJ+ internal editorial communications are not available; editorial intent is inferred from content patterns.
  • Comparative analysis with other digital news outlets (NowThis, Vice News, etc.) was not conducted; whether AJ+'s anti-American content volume is anomalous relative to progressive digital media generally remains an open question.
  • View count authenticity is complicated by the Cyabra finding of 32% fake engagement profiles. The extent to which bot amplification inflates reported view counts cannot be precisely determined.
  • Instagram view counts are null for some posts (particularly older posts and very recent ones), creating partial gaps in aggregate reach calculations. For example, several of the highest-liked Iran war posts have null view counts.
  • Pre-2023 Twitter data is sparse (only 2 tweets from 2020–2021), meaning the 2020 election period is not covered by primary social media data.
  • YouTube and TikTok data — AJ+ is primarily a video platform, but the dataset does not include YouTube-specific metrics. AJ+'s viral impact on these platforms is untracked.
  • Arabic-language AJ+ — Only the English @ajplus account is tracked. AJ+ Arabic (a separate, often more extreme outlet) is not in the dataset.

06 — Method

Methodology Note

This report is based on analysis of AJ+'s publicly available social media output across Twitter/X (13,930 posts, November 2016–April 2026) and Instagram (4,107 posts, April 2023–April 2026), supplemented by independent journalism, U.S. government filings, and think tank analyses. Data was last refreshed on April 20, 2026. Social media metrics (views, likes, retweets, comments) reflect publicly reported platform data. Content analysis focused on posts with the highest engagement metrics and those published during identifiable U.S. political events, including the October 2023 Gaza escalation, the 2024 U.S. presidential election, the Biden–Trump transition, and the 2026 U.S.–Israeli strikes on Iran. Thematic categorization was performed through review of post text and captions, supplemented by semantic search across the full dataset. The report distinguishes between factual findings (metrics, dates, quotes) and interpretive assessments (editorial intent, strategic timing), and attributes advocacy-sourced characterizations to their originating organizations.
